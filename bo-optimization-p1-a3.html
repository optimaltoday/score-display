Audit 3 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Fixed costs are the most dangerous category in any business.

Variable costs respond to revenue. If sales drop, most variable costs drop with them. Fixed costs do not. Rent continues. Payroll continues. Retainers continue. Every fixed cost that was justified at last year's revenue level sits on your balance sheet unchanged — regardless of what this year's revenue looks like.

This audit does not ask whether your fixed costs are large. It asks whether each one is currently justified. A cost justified during a growth period is not automatically justified during a flat or contracting period. Businesses that do not ask this question regularly discover the answer at the worst possible moment.

Audit 3 asks it now — while you can still act on the answer.

Audit 3 Diagnosis
Overhead

Eight dimensions of fixed cost governance — overhead visibility, individual cost review cadence, fixed cost as percentage of revenue, scalability under contraction, legacy cost accumulation, elimination potential, staffing alignment, and space cost proportionality.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your fixed cost base has not been reviewed as a system. Individual costs may be known. The structure — what they collectively consume, how they respond to revenue movement, which ones are legacy and which are current — is not mapped.

This is the highest-risk position in the OPTIMIZATION Pillar. Variable cost problems can be corrected quickly. Fixed cost problems cannot. By the time a bloated fixed cost base becomes visible as a crisis, the correction window is already narrow.

What to do this week

List every fixed cost. Calculate each as a percentage of last month's revenue. If fixed overhead exceeds 40% of gross revenue, this is your most urgent structural priority — above every other initiative on your desk.

Within 14 days

Identify every fixed cost added more than two years ago that has not been reviewed since. These are your highest-probability reduction targets. They were justified under conditions that no longer exist.

Within 30 days

Build a fixed cost reduction plan. Every item gets a decision — keep, reduce, or eliminate — a responsible party, and a deadline. A plan without those three elements is not a plan.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Overhead awareness exists. You know roughly what your fixed costs are. What you have not done is systematically justified each one against current conditions, tested the structure against a revenue contraction, or separated legacy costs from genuinely current ones.

What to do this week

For each fixed cost, ask one question: if this disappeared tomorrow, what would actually stop working? Anything where the honest answer is not much is a reduction candidate. Start there.

Within 14 days

Model a contraction scenario. What happens to your fixed cost coverage if revenue drops 20%? Then 30%? If the picture is uncomfortable, address it now — not during the contraction.

Within 30 days

Separate legacy costs from current costs. Any cost added under different conditions more than two years ago requires re-justification under current conditions. Not assumption — justification.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your overhead is governed, reviewed, and aligned to current operational need. Fixed costs are proportionate, justified, and stress-tested. This is the correct foundation for a resilient business — one where a revenue contraction does not immediately threaten the structure.

You have completed Phase 1 with a strong operational foundation. Phase 2 goes deeper — from awareness to measurement, from knowing what exists to quantifying precisely what each gap costs.

What to do now

Document your overhead review process. A structured review that happens because you remember to do it is not a system. Formalize the cadence, the criteria, and the owner. Then it runs without you.
