Phase 3 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 3 is Diagnosis. Audit 7 examined the internal training architecture. Audit 8 examines the commercial value of what the MULTIPLICATION Pillar is building toward: intellectual property as a distinct, valueable, transferable asset.

Most business owners think of their business value as a function of their operational revenue — a multiple of earnings. IP valuation introduces a different dimension: the value of the knowledge and systems that generate that revenue, assessed independently of the revenue itself. That value can be significant — and it is almost never captured without deliberate structuring.

Audit 8 Context
Why This Audit Matters

IP valuation is not an accounting exercise. It is a commercial positioning exercise.

When you know what your IP is worth — as a licensed methodology, as a franchised system, as a proprietary process — you negotiate from a position of informed authority rather than intuition. You price licensing agreements correctly. You represent business value accurately in a sale. You structure partnership arrangements that reflect the actual contribution of your knowledge, not just your time.

Most business owners who have never valued their IP significantly underprice it in every commercial interaction. The valuation does not just produce a number. It produces a commercial position.

Audit 8 Diagnosis
IP Valuation

Eight dimensions of IP valuation — asset inventory completeness, protection coverage, comparable licensing benchmarks, income generation potential by asset, replacement cost assessment, market demand evidence, exclusivity premium, and valuation methodology selection.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your IP has never been formally valued. You are operating with significant knowledge assets that have no assigned commercial worth — which means they are systematically underpriced in every interaction where they are relevant. Licensing deals, partnership agreements, business sale conversations, and investor discussions all proceed without the anchor of a formal IP valuation. The result is that value is consistently left on the table.

What to do this week

Conduct an informal IP inventory valuation. For each IP asset you identified in Audit 1 and protected in Audit 4: what would it cost a competitor to develop an equivalent capability from scratch? That replacement cost is a floor for the asset's value. It is not a market price — but it is a starting reference that is far more useful than no reference at all.

Within 14 days

Research comparable licensing deals in your sector. What do others charge for access to equivalent methodologies, systems, or frameworks? Licensing benchmarks provide market-based evidence of value that is more compelling in a commercial negotiation than any internally generated estimate.

Within 30 days

Engage an IP specialist or business valuation advisor for a preliminary IP assessment. Even an informal assessment produces a defensible reference point for commercial negotiations — and it often reveals value in assets that the owner had not considered significant.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

IP value is intuitively recognized but not formally quantified. You know your knowledge assets are worth something. You have not established what that something is with enough precision to use it as commercial leverage.

What to do this week

Build a commercial model for your highest-value IP asset. If you licensed it to ten businesses in your sector at a fee you believe is fair, what would the annual income be? That model — even if approximate — gives you a basis for structuring a real licensing conversation.

Within 14 days

Assess your IP value in the context of a business sale. If you were selling your business today, how much of the purchase price would an informed buyer attribute to your IP versus your operational revenue? If the answer is very little — your IP is not yet structured to carry commercial value in a sale. That is a MULTIPLICATION gap with a specific correction.

Within 30 days

Document the evidence of your IP's value. Client outcomes. Performance improvements. Results achieved through your methodology. Commercial evidence of value is the foundation of a defensible IP valuation — and it is evidence you are already generating, even if you have not been collecting it systematically.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your IP has been assessed for commercial value. You have a defensible reference point for licensing negotiations, partnership discussions, and business sale representations. The opportunity at this level is in realization — converting the assessed value into actual income through a structured licensing or partnership program.

What to do now

Identify the one IP asset with the clearest path to licensing income. Map the three to five most likely licensees — businesses that would benefit most from access to your methodology, framework, or system. The licensing conversation begins with a specific asset, a specific licensee profile, and a specific commercial proposition.
