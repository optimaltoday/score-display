Audit 4 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Intellectual property is the legal form of knowledge value.

Knowledge that is valuable but unprotected is available to whoever chooses to use it. Knowledge that is protected — through trademark, copyright, registered methodology, or trade secret documentation — is owned. And ownership is the prerequisite for licensing, for sale, and for defensible valuation.

Most ASEAN family businesses have significant IP that is entirely unprotected. Trade names are unregistered. Processing methods are undocumented as trade secrets. Branded methodologies carry no formal ownership. The knowledge exists and produces value — but it does so without any legal architecture to protect, monetize, or transfer that value.

This audit identifies the gap between the IP you have and the IP you own.

Audit 4 Diagnosis
IP Protection

Eight dimensions of IP protection — trademark registration status, copyright documentation, process and methodology ownership, trade secret formal documentation, domain and digital asset ownership, licensing agreement existence, IP audit recency, and protection coverage relative to commercial value.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your business's intellectual property is substantially unprotected. The methods, frameworks, brand elements, and knowledge assets that differentiate your business are available for competitors to replicate, for partners to use without compensation, and for buyers to discount in a valuation — because they cannot be owned, transferred, or defended in their current form.

This is a commercial gap with a measurable cost. A business with documented, protected IP is worth more. It generates more. And it has options — licensing, franchising, partnership — that an unprotected business simply does not have.

What to do this week

List every element of your business that you believe is genuinely distinctive: your brand name, your service methodology, your product formulation, your process system. Then mark each: trademarked? Copyrighted? Formally documented as a trade secret? The unmarked items are your IP protection gaps.

Within 14 days

Prioritize your trademark registration. Your business name and any distinctive brand element should be registered in every country you operate in or intend to operate in. This is the most fundamental IP protection and the one with the lowest cost relative to its protective value.

Within 30 days

Engage an IP attorney for a one-hour consultation on your top three IP assets. The cost of the consultation is trivial against the licensing income that properly protected IP can generate. The consultation will tell you what is protectable, what is already protected without your knowledge, and what the correct sequence of actions is.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some IP protection exists. Certain brand elements or processes are formally protected. The gap is in coverage — the commercially most valuable IP elements that remain unprotected, informally held, or inadequately documented as trade secrets.

What to do this week

Audit your existing IP registrations against your actual business assets. Which registrations are current? Which have lapsed? Which cover the markets you now operate in versus only the markets you operated in when they were filed? The gap between current registrations and current operations is your immediate protection priority.

Within 14 days

Identify your highest-value unprotected IP. This is typically a methodology, a framework, a process system, or a brand element that competitors could replicate without legal consequence. Document it formally as a trade secret — this provides legal protection without registration and can be converted to registered IP later.

Within 30 days

Establish an IP review cycle. Every 18 months, review all IP registrations for currency, coverage, and commercial relevance. IP protection that is not maintained is IP protection that decays.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your IP is substantially protected. Key brand elements, methodologies, and knowledge assets are formally owned and defensible. The opportunity at this level is in monetization — converting protection into income through licensing, franchising, or structured partnership arrangements that were not possible before the protection existed.

What to do now

Map your protected IP against licensing potential. For each protected asset: who would pay to use this? In what format? Under what terms? A protected IP asset without a licensing strategy is protection without return. The strategy converts protection into income.
