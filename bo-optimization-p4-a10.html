Audit 10 addresses productivity — the output your business generates relative to its inputs. Productivity improvement is the fastest-return operational change available: it does not require new investment, new staff, or new systems. It requires measurement, visibility, and the removal of frictions that suppress output in existing resources.

Audit 10 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Productivity is the ratio that determines whether growth is profitable or merely busy.

A business that grows revenue by 20% while growing headcount by 25% is not more productive — it is more expensive. A business that grows revenue by 20% with the same headcount has improved productivity. The difference compounds. Over five years, the productive business generates significantly more free cash flow from the same revenue base.

Most family businesses do not measure productivity at all. Output is measured — sales, units, transactions. Input is measured — payroll, hours, cost. The ratio between them is rarely calculated, and rarely used to make decisions about where to invest, where to cut, and where to expect return.

This audit builds that ratio and uses it to identify where productivity improvement is most immediately available.

Audit 10 Diagnosis
Productivity

Eight dimensions of productivity measurement — revenue per employee, output per operational hour, high-value versus low-value time allocation, rework and error rate, meeting time as a percentage of operational time, productivity trend over 12 months, incentive alignment with output, and productivity by function or department.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your business's productivity is unmeasured. You know what goes in — the people, the cost, the time. You know what comes out — the revenue, the units, the transactions. The ratio between them has never been calculated systematically, which means productivity improvement has never been targeted specifically.

This is the starting point for most businesses that have grown primarily through effort rather than efficiency. The correction is measurement — not as an end in itself, but as the prerequisite for every subsequent improvement.

What to do this week

Calculate your revenue per employee for the past 12 months. Compare it to your sector benchmark — available through industry associations or comparable listed companies. The gap between your number and the benchmark is your productivity improvement target.

Within 14 days

Track time allocation for your highest-cost staff members for two weeks. Categorize each hour: direct output production, support and administration, meetings, or rework. The distribution will show where productivity leaks — and where the highest-return improvements are.

Within 30 days

Identify your highest-productivity and lowest-productivity operational activities — the things your business does where the return on time and cost is highest and lowest. Shift resource allocation toward the former and reduce time spent on the latter.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some productivity measurement exists. Key output metrics are tracked. The gap is in the ratio — the systematic comparison of output to input that identifies where resources are deployed with the lowest return.

What to do this week

Identify your highest-cost operational activity that does not directly produce revenue. Calculate what it costs annually. Ask: if this activity were cut by 30%, what revenue impact would result? If the answer is none or minimal, that activity is a productivity drain.

Within 14 days

Benchmark your meeting time. What percentage of your team's working hours is spent in meetings? For most businesses the answer is higher than expected — and most meetings produce decisions that could be made faster through a documented decision framework.

Within 30 days

Introduce one productivity metric for each functional area of your business. Not a comprehensive scorecard — one metric per area that the team reviews weekly. What gets measured gets improved.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Productivity is measured and actively managed in your business. Output-to-input ratios are tracked. Resources are deployed with intentionality. You are generating strong returns from your operational base.

At this level, the question is compounding — how to make the next unit of investment produce more than the last one did.

What to do now

Model your productivity at current trajectory for three years. If the trend continues, where does it put you relative to competitors? If the trend is flat — identify the friction that is preventing improvement despite strong measurement. The friction is usually in process or incentive structure, not in effort.
