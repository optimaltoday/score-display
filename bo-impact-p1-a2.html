Phase 1 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 1 is Awareness. Audit 1 examined whether your brand impression is consistent. Audit 2 examines what that impression actually says.

Positioning is the commercial heart of the IMPACT Pillar. It determines who seeks you out, who can be convinced to work with you, and what price they are willing to pay. A business with strong positioning attracts the right clients, repels the wrong ones, and commands prices that reflect value rather than cost. A business with weak positioning attracts anyone, exhausts itself on clients who were never right, and competes on price because it has given the market no other basis for comparison.

Audit 2 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Positioning is not about describing what you do. It is about making the right person feel that you are the only correct option for their specific situation.

That feeling is produced by three elements working together: a clear statement of who you serve, a precise articulation of the problem you solve for them, and a differentiated reason why you solve it better than any alternative. When all three are present and consistent, positioning works. When any one is absent or vague, the business competes on price — because it has given the market no other basis for decision.

This audit examines whether your positioning has all three elements — and whether they are communicated clearly enough to create the commercial attraction they are designed to produce.

Audit 2 Diagnosis
Positioning Audit

Eight dimensions of positioning clarity — target client specificity, problem articulation precision, differentiation clarity, value proposition distinctiveness, competitive comparison strength, positioning consistency across channels, ideal client attraction rate, and wrong-client rejection rate.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your positioning is generic. Your business communicates what it does but not why that matters specifically to the client you most want, why you are the right choice over alternatives, or what the cost of not choosing you actually is. The result is a commercial position that attracts attention without creating conviction — and conviction is what converts attention into revenue at premium pricing.

What to do this week

Write your positioning in one sentence using this structure: We help [specific client] who [specific problem] to [specific outcome] — unlike [alternative] which [key difference]. If you cannot complete this sentence with specific, non-generic answers, your positioning is not yet defined. That sentence is the starting point for everything in this Pillar that follows.

Within 14 days

Test your positioning on five people who represent your ideal client. Read them your positioning sentence. Ask: does this describe you? Does this describe a problem you have? Would this make you want to know more? Honest answers reveal whether your positioning resonates with the people it is designed for.

Within 30 days

Apply your positioning sentence to your three highest-frequency client touchpoints. Your website headline. Your introduction in a meeting. Your proposal opening. Consistent positioning across these three touchpoints produces an immediate improvement in client quality and reduces price negotiation.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Your positioning has directional clarity but lacks the precision that creates commercial conviction. Your target client is generally defined. Your differentiation is partially articulated. The gap is in the specificity — the precise, non-generic language that makes the right client feel immediately understood and the wrong client immediately disqualified.

What to do this week

Sharpen your target client definition. Move from a category — family businesses, property developers, food manufacturers — to a profile: the specific type of business, at the specific stage, facing the specific problem that your offering is designed to solve. The sharper the profile, the more precisely the positioning can speak to it.

Within 14 days

Audit your differentiation claim. What do you say makes you different? Now ask: could a competitor say the same thing honestly? If yes — it is not differentiation. Differentiation must be specific, defensible, and genuinely yours. Find the element of your approach that competitors cannot honestly claim and make that the center of your positioning.

Within 30 days

Introduce a client qualification question. In your first interaction with a prospective client, ask one question that determines whether they are genuinely right for your business. Strong positioning creates the confidence to disqualify. Disqualification improves the quality of every client you accept.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your positioning is clear and commercially directed. The right clients recognize themselves in it. The wrong clients self-select out. The opportunity at this level is in pricing — ensuring the precision of your positioning is matched by pricing that reflects the value it creates rather than the cost it covers.

What to do now

Review your pricing against your positioning. If your positioning claims premium outcomes, your pricing must reflect premium value. A gap between premium positioning and moderate pricing undermines the positioning's credibility — and signals to the market that the premium claim is aspirational rather than demonstrated.
