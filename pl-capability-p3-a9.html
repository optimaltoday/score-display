Expertise has been inventoried and measured. Phase 3 examines the structural conditions that govern how that expertise produces value: whether it can be taught, whether it is priced at its true level, and whether it can be packaged into a form that generates return beyond direct personal delivery.

Audit 9 Context
Why This Audit Matters

A programme is the highest-leverage form of expertise deployment: it packages professional knowledge into a structured experience that can be delivered to multiple participants simultaneously, priced per participant rather than per hour, and reproduced without the linear scaling of the professional's personal time. Not all expertise has programme potential — but expertise that does, and that has not been packaged, represents a significant structural underutilisation of professional capital.

This audit assesses whether your expertise has the structural properties of programme potential and whether the market conditions exist to make a packaged offering commercially viable.

Audit 9 Diagnosis
Program Potential

Eight dimensions of program potential — expertise packageability, curriculum architecture viability, market demand evidence, participant learning arc design, delivery mechanism options, pricing architecture, scalability ceiling, and programme readiness assessment.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

The programme potential of your expertise has not been assessed. The question of whether what you know could be packaged into a structured offering — delivered to groups, priced per participant, reproduced without linear scaling of your personal involvement — has not been examined. This is not a failure of ambition; it is a structural gap in the CAPABILITY architecture that may or may not represent a significant opportunity.

The first step is assessment: whether the expertise has the properties that make programme design viable, and whether the market conditions exist to make a packaged offering commercially sustainable. Those two questions — not the design of the programme itself — are the immediate structural priority.

What to do this week

Assess the packageability of your expertise against three criteria. First, transformability: does your expertise produce a consistently replicable result — one that can be described as a learning outcome and achieved through a structured experience? Second, groupability: can the transformation be delivered to multiple participants simultaneously without significant quality loss? Third, market density: are there enough professionals or organisations who need this transformation to sustain a viable offering?

Within 14 days

If the packageability assessment is positive: map the minimum viable programme architecture. What is the problem it solves? Who specifically is it designed for? What does the participant need to know or be able to do by the end? What is the structure of the experience that produces that outcome? This minimum viable architecture does not need to be complete — it needs to be specific enough to test.

Within 30 days

If the packageability assessment is positive: identify one testing mechanism — a pilot session, a small-group workshop, a webinar, a structured consultation series — that would allow you to test the programme architecture with real participants at minimal development investment. The structural intelligence from a real test is worth more than any amount of desk design.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some informal programme elements exist. You have delivered group sessions, workshops, or structured training. But the architecture is ad hoc — built for specific occasions rather than designed as a replicable, market-facing product. The informal programmes generate value but are not scalable, systematically marketed, or priced at the level a structured, branded offering would command.

What to do this week

Review your existing informal programme activity against three structural questions. Is it replicable in its current form — can it be delivered again at the same quality without significant rebuilding? Is it priced as a programme or as a consulting session? And is it positioned as a product with a defined audience, a defined outcome, and a defined price — or as a service customised to each occasion?

Within 14 days

Identify the informal programme element with the highest replication potential — the one closest to being a structured, repeatable, market-facing product. What is the specific gap between its current state and that product status? Is it documentation, is it branding, is it pricing architecture, is it marketing? Name the gap specifically.

Within 30 days

Close the identified gap for your highest-potential informal programme. The structural objective is to move one offering from ad hoc to architecturally designed: named, documented, priced as a product, and positioned for a defined audience. The first structured programme is the proof of concept for the packaging of your expertise.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Programme architecture is present and active. At least one structured offering exists, is positioned in the market, and is generating professional return. The opportunity at this level is in programme portfolio development — examining whether the current programme architecture represents the full range of your expertise's packaging potential, or whether adjacent offerings could be developed to serve different audience segments or different stages of the learning arc.

What to do now

Map your programme portfolio potential: given the full scope of your expertise inventory, what is the complete range of structured offerings that could be developed? Which serve different audience segments? Which serve different stages of professional development in your market? Which are adjacent to your current offering and could be built with minimal additional development? The portfolio map is the long-range CAPABILITY architecture — your current programme is one point on it.
