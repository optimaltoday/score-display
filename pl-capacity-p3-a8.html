Audit 8 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Role ambiguity is one of the most expensive and least recognised forms of capacity leakage. When the boundaries of your professional role are not architecturally clear — what you are accountable for, what decisions you own, what work belongs elsewhere — capacity flows into the wrong places.

Work that belongs to adjacent functions enters your time and attention because there is no structural mechanism to redirect it. Decisions that others should own require your involvement because accountability is unclear. The result is a professional capacity constantly consumed by work that does not produce the return it should. This audit examines whether your role is architecturally defined with sufficient clarity to protect your capacity for its highest use.

Audit 8 Diagnosis
Role Clarity

Eight dimensions of role clarity — accountability definition precision, decision ownership clarity, function boundary accuracy, role communication quality, adjacent work redirection structure, accountability overlap assessment, role scope management, and function priority alignment.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Role boundaries are not architecturally present. The scope of the professional function — what it includes, what it excludes, and what constitutes work that belongs elsewhere — has not been precisely defined. As a result, capacity is available to every demand that presents itself as relevant, urgent, or relational.

The structural consequence is a professional function whose capacity is allocated by proximity and pressure rather than by design. The work that consumes the most capacity is not necessarily the work that produces the highest value — it is the work that arrives most insistently or from the most difficult-to-decline sources.

What to do this week

Map your last ten working days against two categories: work within your core professional function (work where your contribution is distinctive, accountable, and directly connected to your highest professional value) and work outside it (work that belongs to a different function, absorbed through proximity, relationship, or structural vacuum). Calculate the percentage of capacity currently consumed by out-of-function work.

Within 14 days

For the highest-volume category of out-of-function work: who should own it structurally? What is the specific mechanism — a redirection, a delegation, an explicit handoff — that would return that work to its proper function? The answer is your first role clarity intervention.

Within 30 days

Implement one structural role boundary: a specific category of work that you will no longer absorb, with a defined redirection mechanism. Communicate it clearly to the relevant parties. The first held role boundary is the structural evidence that the architecture can function — and it will release capacity for the work it was protecting.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Role boundaries exist in intention but are not structurally enforced. The core function is understood, but the edges are permeable — adjacent work enters because the mechanism for redirecting it yields under the conditions that most frequently produce it: relational pressure, apparent urgency, or the path of least institutional resistance.

What to do this week

Identify the specific type of out-of-function work that enters your capacity most frequently. What is the structural condition that allows it to enter — the absence of a specific redirection mechanism, a particular relationship dynamic, the absence of a clear alternative owner? That condition is your structural target.

Within 14 days

Build one explicit redirection mechanism for your highest-frequency out-of-function work category. The mechanism needs to be structural — not a preference expressed once, but a consistent practice that applies every time that category of work presents itself.

Within 30 days

Measure the capacity released by holding the role boundary over 30 days. How many hours per week were returned to within-function work? That measurement is both the evidence of the intervention's structural value and the argument for extending the boundary architecture to additional out-of-function categories.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Role architecture is clear and functional. Capacity is protected for within-function work. Out-of-function absorption is structurally prevented. The opportunity at this level is in role evolution — examining whether the current role definition accurately reflects the highest-value professional contribution you are now capable of delivering, and whether the capacity architecture is appropriately concentrated on the work where your function is most distinctive.

What to do now

Examine the current definition of your professional role against your professional trajectory. Are there elements of your current role that could be restructured, delegated, or redefined to concentrate your capacity more precisely on your highest-contribution function? Role clarity is not a fixed architectural element — it requires periodic recalibration as professional capability evolves.
