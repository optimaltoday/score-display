Phase 3 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 3 is Diagnosis. The structural patterns that determine how expertise is deployed, transferred, and valued are rarely examined — which is why they persist.

Expertise has been inventoried and measured. Phase 3 examines the structural conditions that govern how that expertise produces value: whether it can be taught, whether it is priced at its true level, and whether it can be packaged into a form that generates return beyond direct personal delivery.

Phase 3 begins with the teaching capacity of your expertise: whether the professional knowledge you hold can be effectively transmitted to others — and whether that transmission is generating the professional leverage that teaching architecture enables.

Audit 7 Context
Why This Audit Matters

The ability to teach your expertise is not a natural extension of the ability to use it. Expert practice and expert teaching are different capabilities — one is the application of knowledge, the other is the architecture of its transfer. A professional who can only deliver their expertise directly is a professional whose impact and income are bounded by personal capacity. A professional who can also teach it has unlocked a fundamentally different ceiling.

Teaching architecture includes the design of how knowledge is transmitted: the sequencing, the frameworks, the deliberate structure of how expertise passes from the professional who holds it to the professional who needs it. This audit examines whether that architecture exists in your professional practice.

Audit 7 Diagnosis
Teaching Architecture

Eight dimensions of teaching architecture — knowledge transfer effectiveness, curriculum design capacity, teaching framework development, expertise sequencing logic, learner outcome clarity, teaching efficiency, transfer scalability, and pedagogical architecture.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your expertise is not yet in transferable form. You apply it with considerable skill. But if asked to teach it — not demonstrate it, but teach it — you would find that the knowledge exists in a form that is inseparable from your own judgment, experience, and contextual pattern recognition. It cannot be extracted from those conditions and placed into another professional's operating system with sufficient fidelity to produce equivalent results.

This is a structural condition, not a personal failing. Most expert practitioners reach a level of unconscious competence in which the expertise operates below the level of explicit articulation. Teaching requires explicit architecture — the deliberate excavation and reconstruction of knowledge into a form that can be understood, practised, and applied by someone who does not yet have the pattern library you have built.

What to do this week

Choose one area of your expertise and attempt to teach it to someone outside your professional context. Not a colleague who would fill the gaps with their own knowledge — someone who genuinely does not know what you know. The specific moments where the transfer fails — where you resort to "you'll understand when you've done it enough times" — are the precise points where your teaching architecture has a structural gap.

Within 14 days

For each structural gap identified in the teaching exercise: what explicit framework, principle, or decision logic would a learner need to have before the teaching could succeed at that point? Building those frameworks is the foundational work of teaching architecture. They do not need to be elegant — they need to be explicit.

Within 30 days

Design a one-hour teaching session on the most transferable element of your core expertise. Structure it as: the concept, the principle behind it, the common misapplication, a worked example, and a practice exercise. Deliver it once. The structural gaps that emerge in that delivery are your next architectural targets.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

You can explain your expertise when asked. The transfer is partial — it works at the conceptual level but not always at the operational level, and it is ad hoc rather than architecturally designed. A learner who receives your expertise through direct explanation and observation will absorb some of it, but the transmission will be incomplete and will depend heavily on the learner's own interpretive capacity rather than on the quality of your teaching structure.

What to do this week

Review the last three times you explained a significant element of your expertise to someone else. How much of what you intended to transmit do you believe they actually received? How much did they need to interpret or fill in from their own existing knowledge? The gap between what you transmitted and what they received is the structural measure of your current teaching architecture quality.

Within 14 days

Identify the single element of your expertise that you most frequently need to explain and that most frequently produces incomplete understanding in the recipient. That element is your highest-priority teaching architecture development target — because it is the knowledge most frequently needing transmission and currently being transmitted most inefficiently.

Within 30 days

Build a structured transmission architecture for that element: a framework, a sequence, a set of explicit principles, worked examples, and a practice exercise. Test it with one learner. The structural quality of the result — not the content, the architecture — tells you whether the teaching design is working.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Teaching architecture is present and functioning. Your expertise can be transmitted effectively. The opportunity at this level is in teaching leverage — whether the teaching capacity you have built is being deployed in ways that generate direct professional return: a programme with fees, a qualification course, a team development architecture, or a published curriculum that extends your intellectual property into a new market.

What to do now

Map the commercial potential of your existing teaching architecture. In what form could it generate direct professional return — a structured programme, a corporate training engagement, a professional qualification curriculum, a published course? Identify the highest-value deployment and the specific structural steps required to build it.
