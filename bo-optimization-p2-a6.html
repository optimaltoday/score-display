Phase 2 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 2 is Measurement. This is its final audit before Phase 3 moves into structural diagnosis.

Audits 4 and 5 measured internal cost dynamics — working capital timing and technology accumulation. Audit 6 turns to the external cost relationship that most directly affects margin in trading, manufacturing, and distribution businesses: supplier cost concentration and negotiation discipline.

For Southern Thai and ASEAN family businesses, supplier relationships are often generational. The same relationships that built the business may now carry pricing that reflects historical loyalty rather than current market rates. That loyalty has value. Unexamined, it also has a cost.

This audit completes Phase 2. After it, you will have a measurement picture across three of the most significant operational cost drivers in your business. Phase 3 uses that picture to diagnose the structural causes.

Audit 6 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Supplier concentration is a margin risk that most businesses underestimate.

When 60% or more of your purchasing flows through one or two suppliers, the negotiating dynamic is inverted. You need them more than they need you — and pricing over time reflects that reality. This is not unique to small businesses. It is a structural condition that compounds quietly until a supply disruption or a price increase forces the conversation the business should have been having annually.

This audit measures three things simultaneously: what your supplier cost structure currently looks like, what your data position is going into a negotiation, and where the highest-value renegotiation opportunities are — given current market conditions and your actual purchase volume.

Most family businesses negotiate reactively, when prices increase. This audit builds the case for proactive negotiation — when you have leverage, not when you need relief.

Audit 6 Diagnosis
Supplier Cost

Eight dimensions of supplier cost governance — supplier concentration ratio, contract review frequency, rate benchmark against market, negotiation cadence, purchase volume data quality, multi-supplier redundancy, payment terms optimization, and total cost of supply chain versus invoice price.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your supplier cost structure has not been reviewed as a negotiation asset. You are likely paying rates established when the relationship began — adjusted only when the supplier raised prices. That is a negotiation posture by default, not by design.

In commodity-adjacent businesses — rubber, food, logistics — a 1–2% improvement in supplier pricing on significant purchase volumes represents significant annual recovery. That improvement is available in most markets when a buyer arrives at the negotiation with volume data, an alternative, and a specific request. Most never do.

What to do this week

Calculate your supplier concentration ratio. What percentage of total purchase spend flows to your top two suppliers? If it exceeds 60%, concentration risk is your first structural priority — before any renegotiation attempt.

Within 14 days

Compile your purchase volume data by supplier for the past 12 months. This is your negotiation asset. A buyer who can state precisely what they purchased, at what price, and what volume growth they represent negotiates from a position of information.

Within 30 days

Approach your highest-spend supplier with a specific request for revised pricing based on volume. Do not wait for their annual review. Initiate the conversation. Most suppliers prefer to retain a buyer at a slightly lower margin than lose them to a competitor.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some supplier relationships are reviewed. Your highest-spend relationships are probably known, and pricing has been discussed at least occasionally. The leak is in the relationships that have never been formally reviewed — and in the absence of a systematic negotiation cadence that treats supplier pricing as a managed cost rather than a fixed one.

What to do this week

List every supplier relationship that has not been formally reviewed in the past 18 months. Each one is a renegotiation opportunity not yet taken. Rank them by annual spend. Start at the top.

Within 14 days

Introduce a minimum of two qualified alternative suppliers for your highest-spend category. You do not need to use them. You need the supplier to know they exist. Competition changes the dynamic without requiring you to switch.

Within 30 days

Establish an annual supplier review calendar. Every significant relationship gets a formal review once a year — not when prices go up, but as a standard practice. This signals to suppliers that you are an active buyer, not a passive one.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your supplier relationships are actively managed. Pricing is reviewed, negotiated, and benchmarked. Concentration is understood and mitigated with alternative relationships. You are operating as an informed buyer, not a passive one.

At this level, the opportunity is in formalizing what you do instinctively — building a supplier management framework that does not depend on your personal memory of when each relationship was last reviewed.

What to do now

Document your supplier negotiation framework. Who reviews which relationship, when, against what benchmarks, and with what authority to commit to new terms. A process that lives in your head is a process that stops when you do.
