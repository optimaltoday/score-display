Audit 7 begins with training architecture — the internal multiplication mechanism. Before expertise can multiply externally through licensing or franchising, it must multiply internally through a structured knowledge transfer system. The training architecture is the foundation of all external multiplication.

Audit 7 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Training architecture is the system that converts individual expertise into organizational capability.

Without it, every new team member begins from zero. Every competence is rebuilt from scratch. Every mistake that has been made before is made again — because the institutional knowledge that would prevent it is locked in the founder's memory, not in a system accessible to the team.

With it, new team members reach competence faster, make fewer errors, and produce more consistently. The founder's time investment in training drops. Team capability scales with headcount rather than requiring proportional founder attention at each new hire.

Training architecture is also the prerequisite for external multiplication. A licensing agreement, a franchise, or a certified methodology requires a training system that can bring others to competence consistently. Without that system, external multiplication is a concept, not a product.

Audit 7 Diagnosis
Training Architecture

Eight dimensions of training architecture — onboarding program structure, knowledge transfer speed, competence milestone definition, training material quality and accessibility, trainer independence from founder, error rate in trained versus untrained staff, knowledge retention measurement, and training system update frequency.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your business has no systematic training architecture. Knowledge transfer happens informally — through observation, osmosis, and correction in the moment. New team members take longer to reach competence than they should. Errors recur because institutional knowledge is not accessible in a form that prevents them. Your personal time is consumed by training that a system should be doing.

What to do this week

Map your current onboarding experience for a new team member. What do they learn in week one? Week two? Week three? If the answer is it depends, you do not have an onboarding program — you have an approximation. Write down what the ideal first three weeks should contain. That is your training program outline.

Within 14 days

Create training material for the single most frequently required competency in your business. Not a comprehensive training manual — one competency, documented to the standard of a usable training guide. Test it with one new or existing team member. Measure how much faster they reach the competency using the guide versus the previous informal approach.

Within 30 days

Define competence milestones for each role in your business. What does week one, month one, and month three competence look like for each position? Milestones create accountability in training — for the trainer and the trainee — and make progress visible rather than assumed.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some training exists. Certain competencies are taught through structured materials or processes. The gap is in coverage and consistency — the knowledge transfer that still depends on the founder's personal involvement, or that produces variable outcomes because the system is incomplete.

What to do this week

Audit your existing training materials for completeness and currency. Can a new team member reach the target competency using only the existing materials, without asking the founder? If not — identify the specific gaps and fill them.

Within 14 days

Measure your training effectiveness. How long does it take a new team member to reach competence in your most critical function? Compare that to what it would take if you trained them personally. The gap between system-trained and founder-trained time is your training architecture quality indicator.

Within 30 days

Build trainer independence. Identify one team member who can deliver training for one competency without your involvement. This is the training architecture's first autonomy milestone — training that runs without the founder is training that can scale.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your training architecture is functional. Knowledge multiplies within your team through a structured, founder-independent system. New people reach competence predictably. The internal multiplication mechanism works.

The opportunity at this level is in external application — converting your internal training architecture into an external product. If your system can bring your own team to competence, it can bring others' teams to competence too. That is the foundation of a licensed training program.

What to do now

Assess your training architecture for external applicability. Which components are specific to your business and which are applicable to anyone in your sector? The externally applicable components are your licensing foundation. Document them separately from the internal-only components.
