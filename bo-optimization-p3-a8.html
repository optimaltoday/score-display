Phase 3 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 3 is Diagnosis. This phase examines the structural causes beneath the cost symptoms measured in Phase 2.

Audit 7 established whether your business's processes are documented and transferable. Audit 8 examines what happens at the point of transfer — the act of delegation itself, the barriers that prevent it, and the architecture required to make it permanent rather than temporary.

Delegation is consistently the highest-leverage operational change a founder can make. Every task effectively delegated frees founder capacity for higher-value work. Every decision effectively transferred reduces the bottleneck that slows operations when the founder is unavailable. The cumulative effect of systematic delegation is a business that scales — rather than one that hits a ceiling at exactly the founder's personal bandwidth.

Audit 8 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Most founders who struggle with delegation are not control-seeking. They are structure-absent.

Delegation fails when the task is handed over without the context required to perform it. When the success criteria are unclear. When the authority to make related decisions has not been transferred. When the feedback mechanism is absent so small errors compound into larger ones before anyone notices.

Effective delegation is not an act — it is an architecture. It requires a clear task definition, transferred authority, a performance standard, a review mechanism, and a consequence structure. Without all five, the delegation fails — and the founder concludes that it is easier to do it themselves. That conclusion is correct in the short term. It is catastrophic in the long term.

This audit examines whether your delegation architecture contains all five components — or whether delegation in your business is an aspiration rather than a functioning system.

Audit 8 Diagnosis
Delegation

Eight dimensions of delegation architecture — task clarity at handover, authority transfer completeness, performance standard definition, review mechanism establishment, staff capability assessment, delegation frequency, recovery from delegation failures, and founder time in strategic versus operational work.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Delegation in your business is either absent or consistently unsuccessful. Tasks that are handed over return to you — because the handover was incomplete, because the authority was not fully transferred, or because the first failure resulted in the task being reclaimed rather than the delegation being improved.

The result is a business that runs on your direct effort. That is sustainable at one scale. It is not sustainable at the next.

What to do this week

Select one task you perform weekly that takes between 30 minutes and 2 hours. Define it completely — what triggers it, what steps it involves, what a successful outcome looks like, and what authority the person performing it needs. Delegate it with all of that context. Done correctly, it stays delegated.

Within 14 days

Establish a weekly 30-minute delegation review. For each task you have delegated, assess: is it being performed to standard? Are there questions that indicate a context gap? Fix the gap — do not reclaim the task.

Within 30 days

Track your time for one week at 30-minute intervals. Categorize each block: strategic, operational, or administrative. Any week where strategic work is less than 30% of your time is a week where delegation is failing — regardless of how busy you feel.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some delegation is functioning. But the tasks that remain with you by default are disproportionately high-value — not because they require your judgment, but because the architecture for transferring them has not been built.

The pattern at this level: delegation works for routine tasks. It breaks down for complex, judgment-intensive, or relationship-dependent tasks — exactly the tasks where freeing your capacity would have the highest return.

What to do this week

Identify the highest-value task you perform personally that could theoretically be delegated. Map the authority, context, and decision framework required for someone else to perform it. The mapping exercise alone will show you what has been preventing the transfer.

Within 14 days

Review your last three delegation failures — tasks that were delegated and returned to you. Identify the common structural element: was it unclear authority? Absent performance standard? Insufficient context? Correct that element across all current delegations.

Within 30 days

Define your non-delegable work explicitly. What genuinely requires your personal judgment or relationships — not just your preference? Everything outside that defined category is delegable. The explicit definition often reveals that the non-delegable list is much shorter than assumed.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your delegation architecture is functional. You are operating at a level of strategic engagement. Tasks are distributed, authority is clear, and performance is monitored without micromanagement.

At this level, the next frontier is succession — not just of tasks, but of judgment. Transferring decision-making judgment is the highest form of delegation and the one most rarely achieved.

What to do now

Identify the one category of decision where your judgment is most difficult to replicate. Begin documenting the principles behind that judgment — not the decisions themselves, but the framework that produces them. This is the beginning of institutional wisdom, not personal expertise.
