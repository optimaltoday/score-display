Audit 6 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Internal influence is the daily expression of professional authority. It is not a formal title — it is the operational reality of whose expertise is sought, whose perspective shapes decisions, whose initiatives attract organisational support. A professional with high external authority but weak internal influence is a professional whose authority is theoretical rather than operational — recognised outside the organisation but not sufficiently leveraged within it.

This audit examines the gap between the professional authority your expertise warrants and the influence you actually exercise in the environments where you operate daily.

Audit 6 Diagnosis
Internal Influence

Eight dimensions of internal influence — decision-shaping capacity, expertise-deference rate, initiative authority, internal network quality, influence reach across functions, authority recognition pattern, internal positioning clarity, and professional standing measurement.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Internal influence is weak relative to the expertise held. Your professional knowledge is present in the organisation or professional community, but it is not translating into the authority to initiate decisions, shape direction, or implement change at the level your expertise warrants.

There is a specific structural reason this gap exists so commonly: internal influence requires both expertise and the deliberate architecture of that expertise's internal communication. A professional who is recognised externally but has not invested in the internal communication of their expertise — through the consistent demonstration of authoritative judgment in internal forums, through the building of internal professional relationships, through the visible articulation of professional position in the internal context — will find that internal influence lags external recognition.

What to do this week

Map your internal influence landscape. In which domains of decision-making within your organisation or professional community is your expertise consistently sought? In which domains where your expertise is most relevant is it not being sought? The gap between the two is your internal influence deficit.

Within 14 days

For the highest-priority internal influence gap — the domain where your expertise is most relevant and least operationally recognised — identify the specific structural reason. Is it because you have not made your expertise visible internally? Because internal relationships have not been built? Because the internal communication of your expertise has been inconsistent? The specific reason determines the appropriate structural response.

Within 30 days

Implement one deliberate internal authority-building action: a contribution to an internal forum that demonstrates the application of your expertise to a live organisational challenge, a direct demonstration of professional judgment in a context where your authority is currently not recognised, or a structured engagement with an internal decision-maker who should be seeking your expertise but currently is not.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Internal influence is partial. In some domains your authority is recognised and exercised effectively. In others — typically domains where your expertise is most recently developed, or where the internal relationships that support authority recognition have not been built — the influence falls short of what your expertise warrants.

What to do this week

Identify the single internal domain where your influence gap is most costly — where the absence of authority in that specific area is producing professional outcomes below what your expertise could deliver if operationally recognised. That domain is your priority internal influence development target.

Within 14 days

Map the internal relationships that most directly affect authority recognition in your priority domain. Whose deference to your expertise in that domain would most significantly change the internal influence dynamic? Build one deliberate engagement with that person or group that demonstrates the application of your expertise to a problem or decision they care about.

Within 30 days

Develop one internal authority signal in your priority domain: a structured contribution, a demonstrated judgment call, or a documented expertise application that becomes a reference point for internal recognition. Internal authority is built through the accumulation of visible expertise applications — not through self-assertion, but through the consistent demonstration of professional judgment at the level that generates deference.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Internal influence is strong and well-exercised. Your expertise is recognised and operationally deferred to in the domains where it is most relevant. The opportunity at this level is in influence architecture extension — whether the internal authority that functions well in your primary domain can be extended to adjacent domains, or whether the professional community that recognises your authority internally can be used as a platform for building broader external authority.

What to do now

Identify the internal authority you have built and the external leverage it could generate. Internal recognition from a well-regarded professional community is one of the most credible sources of external authority — because it represents peer validation by those who have directly observed professional performance. How can the internal authority architecture you have built be deliberately converted into external professional recognition?
