Phase 3 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 3 is Diagnosis. The structural patterns that produce professional authority — or undermine it — are not always visible from within.

The dimensions of authority have been examined and measured. Phase 3 moves to the patterns that govern how authority is expressed, received, and extended: whether the digital presence reinforces or undermines the professional signal, whether communication quality matches the authority being claimed, and whether recognition is confined to a geography or professional context that limits the ceiling of what authority can produce.

Phase 3 begins with the most pervasive dimension of modern professional authority architecture: the digital presence — the set of digital signals that represent your professional authority to the professional world, often before any direct relationship has been established.

Audit 7 Context
Why This Audit Matters

For most professionals today, the digital presence is the first encounter point — the place where potential clients, referrers, employers, and peers form an initial impression of professional authority before any direct engagement occurs. A digital presence that does not match the authority level a professional holds in person creates a gap that works against the authority architecture at its most critical point: the moment of first evaluation.

This audit examines whether your digital professional presence is reinforcing your authority architecture or creating a structural inconsistency that works against it.

Audit 7 Diagnosis
Digital Professional Presence

Eight dimensions of digital professional presence — primary platform quality, content authority signalling, profile completeness and currency, digital positioning coherence, published intellectual content quality, online reputation architecture, digital reach measurement, and platform selection appropriateness.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

The digital professional presence is not aligned with your professional authority. The primary digital touchpoints — professional profile, published content, online reputation — are either absent, outdated, or inconsistent with the authority level your expertise represents.

The structural cost is invisible but significant: every time a potential client, referrer, or opportunity-holder encounters your digital presence before any direct engagement, they form an authority impression from the digital signal alone. If that signal is weak or absent, the authority architecture has a structural gap at the most frequent and most formative first-contact point.

What to do this week

Audit your primary digital professional presence from the perspective of someone who has never met you. Search your name in the professional context most relevant to your expertise. What impression does the result create? Does it communicate authority, or does it communicate absence? Rate the authority signal on a 1–5 scale from the perspective of a potential client who knows nothing about you except what they find digitally.

Within 14 days

Identify the single highest-priority digital presence improvement — the one element that, if addressed, would produce the most significant improvement in the authority signal created by your digital footprint. For most professionals, this is the primary professional profile (LinkedIn, professional website, or firm biography). Make that one improvement this week.

Within 30 days

Build a minimum viable digital authority architecture: a current, complete, and authority-level professional profile on the single most professionally relevant platform for your expertise and audience. The architecture does not need to be elaborate — it needs to be accurate, current, and of a quality standard consistent with the authority level you hold in person.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Digital presence is partially developed. Some elements are strong and coherent with your authority positioning. Others are outdated, absent, or inconsistent — creating a fragmented digital impression that is less than the sum of its parts. The professional who encounters your strongest digital touchpoint forms an authority impression; the professional who encounters a weaker one forms a different one.

What to do this week

Identify the single largest inconsistency between your strongest digital touchpoint and a weaker one. What specifically is producing the inconsistency — outdated information, a different quality standard of presentation, the absence of content that your strongest touchpoint includes? The specific inconsistency is the structural target.

Within 14 days

Bring the weakest digital touchpoint up to the standard of the strongest. Not necessarily in complexity or volume — in quality standard, currency, and authority-coherent positioning. The structural objective is consistency: every digital encounter with your professional presence should produce an equivalent authority impression.

Within 30 days

Identify one opportunity to add active digital authority signalling — a published piece, a professional contribution, a structured content that demonstrates the application of your expertise to a problem your target audience cares about. Active digital authority is not a profile — it is evidence of professional judgment available for public evaluation.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Digital professional presence is strong, current, and coherent with the authority architecture. The opportunity at this level is in digital authority amplification — whether the digital presence is actively generating professional opportunity, or whether it is functioning as a reference point for those who already know you rather than as a discovery mechanism for those who do not.

What to do now

Assess the active return your digital presence is generating: how many professional opportunities — client enquiries, speaking invitations, referral introductions, publication requests — were initiated through a digital encounter in the past 12 months? If the number is low relative to the quality of the digital presence, the presence is built but not optimised for discovery. The next structural question is whether the presence can be made more discoverable — through search-optimised content, through platform engagement, or through deliberate digital network extension.
