Phase 1 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 1 is Awareness. Before capacity can be structured, its current state must be seen with precision.

The CAPACITY Pillar addresses the foundational resource that determines everything a professional or leader can produce: personal capacity. Unlike financial capital or organisational resources, capacity operates invisibly — it is consumed daily without measurement, eroded gradually without alarm, and exhausted suddenly without warning. Most professionals operate with no structural view of their own capacity at all. Phase 1 establishes that view.

Phase 1 continues with the fuel that determines whether time — however well protected — is actually productive: professional energy, and how it is currently managed, deployed, and recovered.

Audit 2 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Time can be protected and still be unproductive. The variable that determines whether protected time produces at its potential is not discipline — it is energy. Professional energy is finite, directional, and non-transferable: cognitive energy deployed in reactive administration is not available for strategic thinking. Physical energy depleted by inadequate recovery is not available for high-stakes performance.

This audit examines whether your professional energy is being structurally managed — or whether it is being spent without architecture, on demand, until it runs out.

Audit 2 Diagnosis
Energy Management

Eight dimensions of energy management — cognitive energy allocation, physical recovery adequacy, energy-value matching, depletion recognition speed, renewal practice frequency, performance consistency across the week, energy expenditure awareness, and recovery architecture.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Professional energy is being consumed without structural management. The day begins with full capacity and ends with deficit — and recovery between working days is insufficient to fully restore what was spent. Performance is being generated from a structural deficit rather than from a restored baseline.

The result compounds over time: the ceiling of what is achievable in a given day declines, decision quality degrades in the second half of the working day, and sustained effort deployed to compensate for depleted energy produces diminishing returns while deepening the structural problem.

What to do this week

Track your energy level four times per day for one week: morning start, mid-morning, mid-afternoon, and end of day. Rate it 1–5. Do not change behaviour — only observe. The pattern you find is your baseline energy architecture. Any consistent collapse below 3 by mid-afternoon is a structural signal, not a personal failing.

Within 14 days

For the time period where energy most consistently collapses: what are you doing in the two hours before that point? Is it high-cognitive-load work, reactive demand, or low-value administration? The activity pattern preceding depletion identifies the structural cause.

Within 30 days

Implement one recovery interval mid-day — a structural break of at least 20 minutes, away from screen and demand, before the energy collapse point. This is not discretionary rest. It is a structural intervention that changes the energy curve for the second half of the working day.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some energy management exists. Recovery practices are present but inconsistent — they hold during normal weeks and collapse during heavy ones, which are precisely when recovery matters most. The result is a performance profile that is strong in lighter periods and significantly degraded under sustained pressure.

What to do this week

Identify the specific conditions under which your recovery practices collapse. Is it when travel increases? When a high-stakes deadline arrives? When a particular type of demand enters the schedule? The conditions of collapse are the structural target — not the general intention to recover better.

Within 14 days

Build one recovery practice that is structurally protected — not dependent on the week being manageable. Sleep duration is the highest-leverage starting point: a protected minimum that holds regardless of workload.

Within 30 days

Assess your energy profile over the past month. Were there identifiable periods of significantly degraded performance? Map them against the recovery practices that were absent or compressed in the preceding days. That correlation is your structural evidence for the intervention you need to make permanent.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Energy management is architecturally sound. Recovery is deliberate, consistent, and sufficient. The opportunity at this level is in precision deployment — not just protecting energy from depletion, but directing the highest-quality energy toward the highest-value work at the times of day when cognitive performance is strongest.

What to do now

Identify the highest-cognitive-demand work in your professional function — the work that requires the most from your thinking at its best. Map when you currently schedule it against when your energy profile is at its daily peak. If they are not aligned, that alignment is your next structural optimisation.
