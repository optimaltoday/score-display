Phase 2 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 2 is Measurement. Authority that has not been measured cannot be strategically developed.

Awareness established the current expression of professional authority. Measurement now quantifies it — examining the specific signals by which authority is conveyed, the market contexts in which it is most and least effective, and the precise gap between the authority level the professional currently projects and the level their expertise warrants.

Phase 2 begins with the most direct market signal of professional authority: pricing. What you charge is not merely an economic decision — it is a professional communication. Fees that are too low undermine the authority signal; fees that are calibrated to the authority level reinforce it. This audit examines whether your pricing architecture is working with your authority strategy or against it.

Audit 4 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Fees are a signal before they are a transaction. When a potential client or employer evaluates your professional authority, your pricing is one of the most powerful pieces of evidence they use — not because high fees guarantee quality, but because pricing that is inconsistent with the authority being claimed creates cognitive dissonance that works against the engagement. A professional who claims authority-level expertise but prices at practitioner rates is sending a contradictory signal that the market resolves by discounting the authority claim.

This audit examines whether your pricing architecture is coherent with your authority positioning — and where the gap between current pricing and authority-level pricing is most significant.

Audit 4 Diagnosis
Pricing and Fee Authority

Eight dimensions of pricing and fee authority — fee level vs. positioning coherence, authority premium capture, pricing confidence, fee communication architecture, negotiation floor clarity, pricing differentiation, market rate vs. authority rate gap, and fee structure design.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your fees are set by convention, negotiation, or historical precedent rather than by authority positioning. The pricing does not reflect the professional authority level your expertise warrants — and the contradiction between the authority you claim and the price you charge is visible to those evaluating you.

There is also a compounding structural effect: pricing at practitioner rates while claiming authority-level expertise creates a credibility gap. Sophisticated clients and employers — precisely the ones most likely to value authority-level expertise — often interpret low pricing as a signal that the authority claim is not matched by market recognition. The pricing is undermining the authority it should be reinforcing.

What to do this week

Calculate the authority premium in your professional market. What do the recognised authorities in your expertise domain charge, relative to competent practitioners? That ratio is your authority premium reference. Compare your current fees against both the practitioner rate and the authority rate. The gap between your current fees and the authority rate is the value you are structurally leaving on the table.

Within 14 days

Identify the single client or engagement context in which you are most confident your expertise delivers authority-level value. Calculate what authority-level pricing would look like for that context specifically. Then identify the structural reason your current pricing is below that level — is it market conditioning, confidence, competitive pressure, or convention? Naming the reason is the prerequisite for addressing it.

Within 30 days

Implement an authority-level price for one new engagement. Not every engagement simultaneously — one. The first time authority-level pricing holds without losing the client or employer relationship is the structural evidence that the market will support it. It is also the first step in the pricing architecture migration from convention-based to authority-based.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Fees are present and defensible, but the authority premium available in your market has not been fully captured. Some pricing awareness exists — you are not charging at the bottom of the market — but the distance between your current pricing and the authority-level pricing your positioning would support has not been deliberately closed.

What to do this week

Identify the last three fee negotiations or compensation discussions you had. In each: what determined the final number? Market convention, client pushback, your own hesitation, or a deliberate authority-based calculation? The decision process tells you whether pricing is being set architecturally or reactively.

Within 14 days

Build a fee rationale document: a structured articulation of why your fees are set at the level they are, grounded in the value your expertise produces rather than in the market convention for your role. This document is not for clients — it is for your own use in fee conversations. A professional who can articulate the structural basis for their pricing has a fundamentally different authority in that conversation than one who is defending a number.

Within 30 days

Test one authority-based pricing increase in a context where the value-production argument is strongest. Use the fee rationale document internally. The structural measure of success is not just whether the fee holds — it is whether the conversation moves from price negotiation to value discussion. That conversation shift is the evidence that the pricing architecture is communicating authority.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Pricing is architecturally aligned with authority positioning. Fees communicate the professional level you occupy and reinforce the authority signal rather than contradicting it. The opportunity at this level is in pricing architecture refinement — whether the current fee structure optimally captures the authority premium across all engagement types and client contexts, or whether specific engagement formats, client categories, or delivery modes are still being priced below their authority potential.

What to do now

Audit your full fee structure across engagement types: advisory, project, retainer, programme, and any other format. For each: is the pricing authority-level for that format specifically? Some professionals who have successfully migrated to authority pricing in one engagement format continue to price at practitioner levels in others. The opportunity is in the formats where the authority premium has not yet been applied.
