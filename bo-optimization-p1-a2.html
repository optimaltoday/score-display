Audit 2 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Every business has costs that exist because they were never stopped.

Not because they are necessary. Not because they produce a return. Because stopping them requires a decision — and the decision was never made. Cost structure audit exists to force that decision. Systematically. Across every category of exit.

This audit does not ask whether your business is profitable. It asks whether every baht exiting your business is earning its place. Those are different questions. A profitable business can carry 10–15% in costs that produce nothing — and never know it, because the profit masks the leak.

Audit 2 removes that mask.

Audit 2 Diagnosis
Cost Structure

Eight dimensions of cost governance — recurring cost visibility, supplier contract review cadence, revenue-generating versus overhead distinction, return measurement, subscription and tool audit, negotiation practice, cost-to-revenue growth comparison, and approval process discipline.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your cost structure is operating on default. Costs were added when they seemed reasonable and have continued without review. No one has asked — systematically — what each cost produces. And because no one has asked, the answer is unknown.

This is the structural definition of money leak. Not one large drain. A dozen small ones that have never been questioned. Individually they look manageable. Collectively they represent a significant percentage of annual revenue exiting without verified return.

What to do this week

Pull your last three bank and card statements. List every recurring charge. Label each one: Revenue-Generating, Operational Necessity, or Unreviewed. This takes 90 minutes. It surfaces the gaps immediately.

Within 14 days

Cancel or pause every Unreviewed cost. If you cannot identify what it produces, it should not continue while you decide.

Within 30 days

Contact your top three suppliers. Ask for revised rates. Most will negotiate when asked directly. Most are never asked.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Partial governance exists. Some costs are tracked and reviewed. Others survive on inertia — continued not because they are necessary but because stopping them was never prioritized.

The risk at this level is complacency. The structure looks functional enough that nothing feels urgent. Meanwhile costs that were justified two years ago under different conditions continue unchanged. Business conditions have changed. The cost structure has not caught up.

What to do this week

Identify every supplier or vendor contract not reviewed in the past 12 months. Each one is a negotiation opportunity not yet taken.

Within 14 days

Build a cost registry. One document. Every recurring cost, its monthly value, its category, its last review date. Update it quarterly. This eliminates most cost drift permanently.

Within 30 days

Identify which costs are growing faster than revenue. Any cost that outpaces revenue growth without documented justification is a structural leak in progress.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your cost structure is largely governed and reviewed. Costs are visible, purposeful, and aligned to current operational need. You are operating with cost discipline that most businesses at your stage have not yet built.

The inertia-driven costs that remain — and there are always some — are your next target. Not urgently. Systematically.

What to do now

Introduce a formal quarterly negotiation cycle. Scheduled. Calendar-blocked. Not reactive. Systematic supplier negotiation recovers 3–8% of annual supplier spend in documented cases. At your cost governance level, that recovery is accessible.
