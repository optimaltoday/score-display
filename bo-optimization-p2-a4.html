Audit 4 begins with working capital — the most directly felt measurement in any operating business. If you have ever been profitable on paper while simultaneously short on cash, this audit names the structure behind that experience.

Audit 4 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Working capital is the gap between money going out and money coming in.

Every business has one. The question is whether that gap is measured, managed, and understood — or whether it appears as a surprise every few months that requires urgent action.

A business can be profitable and cash-constrained simultaneously. Inventory sitting in a warehouse is money that has left the bank but not yet returned. Receivables outstanding past 60 days are money that has been earned but not collected. Payables due before collections arrive are timing gaps that consume cash without consuming profit. None of these show as losses. All of them cost money.

Audit 4 measures your cash conversion cycle and the structural gaps within it. The number it surfaces — how many days of cash are locked in your operating cycle — is one of the most commercially significant numbers in your business. Most owners do not know it.

Audit 4 Diagnosis
Working Capital

Eight dimensions of working capital management — cash conversion cycle visibility, receivables aging, inventory turnover, payables terms, seasonal cash pattern awareness, cash flow forecasting, working capital as a percentage of revenue, and financing cost of working capital gaps.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your working capital position is unmanaged. Cash flow surprises are a regular feature of your operating reality — not because your business is unprofitable, but because the timing of cash in and cash out has never been mapped.

Every day that receivables sit uncollected beyond your terms is a day you are financing your customers with your own capital — at zero interest, by default. Every week that inventory sits unmoved is a week of working capital locked without return. These are not accounting problems. They are structural ones.

What to do this week

Calculate your current receivables aging. How much is outstanding beyond 30 days? Beyond 60? That number is your immediate working capital recovery target.

Within 14 days

Implement a collections follow-up sequence for all receivables beyond 30 days. Phone call at 31 days. Written notice at 45. Escalation at 60. Make this a system, not a reaction.

Within 30 days

Calculate your inventory turnover rate. Any inventory sitting beyond your sector's average holding period is working capital that should be liquid. Identify the highest-value slow-moving stock and address it.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some measurement exists. You have a general sense of your cash cycle. What you lack is the precise number — how many days of revenue are locked in your operating cycle at any given moment — and the system to reduce it.

At this level, the leak is in the gaps. Receivables that almost always collect within terms — except when they do not. Inventory that turns reasonably well — except for the slow-moving categories that tie up disproportionate capital.

What to do this week

Calculate your Days Sales Outstanding — total receivables divided by average daily revenue. Compare it to your stated payment terms. The gap is money you are owed but have not collected.

Within 14 days

Review your payables terms with your top three suppliers. If you are paying in 30 days and could negotiate 45, that 15-day extension on your highest invoices may free significant working capital.

Within 30 days

Build a 13-week cash flow forecast. Not a budget — a forecast of actual cash in and cash out by week. This is the single most powerful working capital management tool available and requires no software beyond a spreadsheet.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your working capital cycle is understood and actively managed. You know your cash conversion cycle. Receivables are tracked. Inventory turns at a rate aligned with your business model. Payables are optimized.

At this level, the opportunity is in acceleration — reducing your cash conversion cycle by days to free working capital that can be redeployed rather than locked in the operating cycle.

What to do now

Calculate the financial impact of reducing your cash conversion cycle by 10 days. On a business with significant revenue, that reduction may free more working capital than your current financing costs. That freed capital can fund growth without additional borrowing.
