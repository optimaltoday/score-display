Audit 10 Context
Why This Audit Matters

A diagnosis without a prescription is an incomplete engagement. The preceding nine audits have examined the dimensions of your professional capacity with structural precision. Phase 4 converts that intelligence into action. A capacity prescription is not a list of good intentions — it is a structured set of specific interventions, sequenced by priority and impact, that closes the gap between the capacity architecture you currently have and the one your professional function requires.

This audit examines whether you have that prescription, and whether you are implementing it with structural commitment rather than aspirational intention.

Audit 10 Diagnosis
Capacity Prescription

Eight dimensions of capacity prescription — intervention identification completeness, priority sequencing, implementation timeline specificity, commitment precision, change tracking, high-impact action clarity, structural change depth, and prescription follow-through rate.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

No structural prescription is in place. The CAPACITY Pillar audits have generated significant diagnostic intelligence — the dimensions of time, energy, attention, boundaries, recovery, and patterns have been examined with precision. But that intelligence has not been converted into a structured, sequenced, committed plan for architectural change.

The cost of prescription absence is that diagnostic intelligence defaults to awareness without consequence: you know more about the structure of your professional capacity than you did before, but the structure itself has not changed. Awareness without prescription is the most expensive form of insight — it consumes the attention required to generate it without producing the structural change that justifies that investment.

What to do this week

From the nine preceding audits, identify the five structural interventions that would produce the highest return on your professional capacity if implemented. Not the most comfortable or the most obvious — the five that would produce the most significant structural improvement. Write them as specific actions, not general intentions.

Within 14 days

Sequence the five identified interventions by two criteria: impact (which produces the most significant structural improvement?) and implementation speed (which can be implemented without depending on external conditions or third-party decisions?). The highest-impact, fastest-to-implement intervention is your first prescription commitment. Begin it this week.

Within 30 days

Build a 90-day capacity prescription: five interventions, sequenced by priority, each with a specific implementation action and a measurable indicator of completion. Review it at 30 days and assess actual versus intended implementation progress. This is not a wish list — it is a structural commitment with the same accountability as a professional deliverable.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some structural changes have been made. The prescription is partial — the most visible or most uncomfortable interventions have been addressed, but the complete architectural change required by the diagnostic picture has not been systematically built or committed to. Some high-cost, low-value structural elements remain in place because the full prescription has not been executed.

What to do this week

Identify the structural interventions from your diagnostic work that have not yet been acted upon. For each: what is the specific reason it has not been implemented — a practical obstacle, a relational complexity, or avoidance of the discomfort of structural change? Name the obstacle explicitly. Obstacles that are named can be addressed; obstacles that are unnamed function as permanent deferrals.

Within 14 days

For the highest-impact unimplemented intervention: build the specific implementation plan. Not the intention — the plan. Who is involved? What is the first action? By what date? What is the measurable indicator that it has been completed? The plan converts the intention from an aspiration to a structural commitment.

Within 30 days

Review your prescription progress at 30 days. For each committed intervention: implemented, partially implemented, or not started? For each not started: is the obstacle structural or avoidance? The answer determines the response — a structural obstacle requires a plan revision; avoidance requires a commitment decision.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

The capacity prescription is in place and being implemented. Structural changes are underway. The opportunity at this level is in prescription quality assurance — examining whether the interventions you have committed to are the highest-impact ones available, or whether the prescription reflects the most visible interventions rather than the most structurally significant ones.

What to do now

Review your capacity prescription against the full diagnostic picture generated by the first nine audits. Are the interventions you are implementing the ones that would produce the largest structural improvement? Or have any high-impact interventions been systematically avoided — because they are more demanding, more relationally complex, or require a more significant professional commitment to execute? Name them.

Audit 11 — Sustainability Architecture
Is your current professional capacity architecture built to perform across the career horizon you intend — or does it contain structural fragility points that will not hold at sustained intensity?

Capacity Prescription built the structural intervention plan for current capacity. Sustainability Architecture examines whether the architecture being built is designed for the distance.
