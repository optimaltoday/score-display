Phase 2 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 2 is Measurement. Each audit in this phase quantifies a specific category of operational cost — the precise financial impact of structural gaps identified in Phase 1.

Audit 4 measured the cost of your cash conversion cycle. Audit 5 measures a different category of cost: the technology and tool spend that accumulates silently in most businesses — added during periods of growth or problem-solving and rarely reviewed thereafter.

Technology spend is one of the fastest-growing cost categories in small and mid-size family businesses over the past five years. It is also one of the least reviewed. The result is a common pattern: significant monthly spend on tools that overlap in function, are used by one or two people, or have been replaced in practice by other solutions but continue to charge.

Audit 5 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Technology costs are uniquely dangerous because they feel small individually and compound collectively.

A SaaS subscription at a modest monthly rate does not feel significant. Twelve such subscriptions at similar price points represent significant annual spend — for tools that may or may not be producing a return. At higher price points, the math compounds faster.

This audit does not ask whether technology is valuable. It asks whether each specific tool your business currently pays for is producing measurable value proportionate to its cost. That is a different question — and most businesses have not asked it systematically.

The second dimension this audit examines is utilization. A tool that is fully paid for and 20% utilized is 80% waste. Identifying underutilized tools is as valuable as identifying unused ones.

Audit 5 Diagnosis
Technology Spend

Eight dimensions of technology spend governance — complete stack inventory, utilization rates, functional overlap, integration effectiveness, cost-per-user analysis, tool consolidation opportunity, renewal review process, and shadow IT exposure.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your technology stack has never been audited as a complete system. Individual tools were added to solve specific problems. Whether those problems are still the same, whether the tool solved them, and whether the tool is still in active use — none of this has been reviewed systematically.

The most common finding in businesses at this level: 20–35% of technology spend is for tools that are either unused, underutilized, or functionally duplicated by another tool already in the stack. That spend produces zero return and could be eliminated in a single audit session.

What to do this week

Pull every technology-related charge from your last three months of bank and card statements. List each tool, its monthly cost, and its primary user. This inventory does not exist anywhere else — build it now.

Within 14 days

For each tool, ask three questions: Is it actively used weekly? Could another tool already in the stack do the same job? Does the cost justify the output? Any tool that fails two of three gets cancelled at its next renewal.

Within 30 days

Establish a renewal review process. Every subscription renewal — regardless of size — gets a 10-minute review before automatic payment. This single process prevents most future accumulation.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some awareness of your technology stack exists. Major tools are known and used. The leak is in the periphery — the smaller subscriptions added for specific purposes that were never reviewed after the purpose was addressed, and the tools whose utilization has declined but whose billing continues.

What to do this week

Identify every tool in your stack used by fewer than 50% of its licensed users. Per-seat costs on underutilized platforms are the most immediately recoverable technology waste.

Within 14 days

Look for functional overlap. Do you have two tools that perform the same core function? Consolidation to the better-utilized tool typically recovers the full cost of the eliminated one and reduces operational complexity simultaneously.

Within 30 days

Negotiate annual terms for any tools you are paying monthly. Annual billing on the same tool typically saves 15–25% with no reduction in functionality.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your technology spend is largely purposeful and audited. Tools are utilized, integrated, and proportionate to their cost. The stack is not bloated.

At this level, the next question is not what to cut — it is what to deepen. Most businesses underutilize the tools they already own. A 20% improvement in utilization of your current stack produces more return than adding a new tool.

What to do now

Identify the one tool in your current stack that, if used at 80% of its documented capability, would most significantly improve an operational output. Invest in training that capability before adding anything new.
