Phase 2 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 2 is Measurement. Audit 3 established the customer perception gap. Audit 4 measures the most direct commercial consequence of that gap: pricing authority.

Pricing authority is the degree to which your business can set and hold prices without negotiation. A business with strong positioning has strong pricing authority — because the client understands specifically what they get, why it is worth the price, and what it costs them to choose a cheaper alternative. A business with weak positioning negotiates on every transaction — because the client has no basis for comparison other than price.

Audit 4 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Price resistance is always a positioning signal — not a market signal.

When clients consistently push back on your prices, the instinctive response is to lower them. The correct response is to sharpen the positioning that justifies them. Clients do not resist prices they understand the full value of. They resist prices that are not anchored to a clear, specific, differentiated outcome that they cannot get elsewhere at an equivalent cost.

This audit measures your pricing authority — not your prices — and identifies the positioning gaps that are suppressing it.

Audit 4 Diagnosis
Pricing Authority

Eight dimensions of pricing authority — price resistance frequency, discount rate and frequency, pricing relative to value delivered, pricing relative to market benchmark, client quality correlation with price acceptance, proposal conversion rate by price tier, price increase success rate, and pricing communication confidence.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your pricing is under structural pressure. Clients negotiate as a matter of course — not because your price is genuinely wrong, but because your positioning does not make it feel self-evident. Every negotiation costs you margin, time, and — most significantly — the psychological authority that allows you to hold your price in future interactions.

In a business with significant revenue, a 5% average discount rate across all transactions represents permanent annual income foregone. That is the minimum cost of pricing authority incoherence at your scale. The actual cost — including the wrong clients you accept at discounted rates and the right clients who walk away assuming you are not premium — is larger.

What to do this week

Calculate your actual average effective price versus your stated price across the last 20 transactions. The gap — the discount rate — is your pricing authority measurement. If it exceeds 5%, your positioning is not strong enough to support your stated pricing. That is not a pricing problem. It is a positioning problem with a pricing symptom.

Within 14 days

Change how you present your price. Price should always follow a clear articulation of the specific value delivered — not precede it. If you are leading with the price and following with the justification, reverse the sequence. The perception of value must be established before the price is named.

Within 30 days

Introduce a price increase for your next three proposals — 10% above your usual rate. Observe the response. If no one pushes back, your current pricing is below your actual pricing authority. If pushback is significant, identify the specific positioning gap that is causing it and close it before the next price increase attempt.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Your pricing holds in most situations. Price resistance occurs but is not the defining feature of your commercial conversations. The gap is in the margin being left on the table by occasional discounting and by pricing that is set conservatively relative to your actual positioning strength.

What to do this week

Review your last five discounted transactions. What was the stated reason for the discount? For each: was the discount a positioning failure — the client did not see sufficient value — or a commercial judgment — the client was strategically important at a lower margin? The distinction determines whether the response is a positioning correction or a pricing strategy adjustment.

Within 14 days

Build a value articulation script for your highest-ticket offering. Before any price conversation, use this script to establish the specific, quantified value the client receives. A client who has been walked through the value of an outcome is far less likely to negotiate the price of that outcome.

Within 30 days

Test your pricing ceiling. Approach your next two proposals with a price 15% above your usual rate. Present the value clearly and hold the price without volunteering a discount. Observe where the ceiling actually is — it is almost always higher than habit has established.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your pricing authority is strong. Clients accept your prices because your positioning makes them feel self-evident. Discounting is rare and strategic rather than defensive. The opportunity at this level is in testing the ceiling — understanding how far your positioning can support price increases before resistance genuinely emerges.

What to do now

Map the relationship between your positioning strength and your pricing ceiling. Which specific positioning elements — which phrases, which demonstrated outcomes, which client testimonials — produce the most significant reduction in price resistance? Those elements are your pricing leverage. Amplify them in every commercial interaction.
