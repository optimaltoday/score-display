Phase 2 Context
Why This Matters Now

Phase 2 is Measurement. This is its final audit before Phase 3 diagnoses the structural causes.

Audits 4 and 5 measured the external commercial consequences of positioning gaps. Audit 6 turns inward: the internal authority dimension of brand coherence — whether the people who represent your business in every interaction understand, internalize, and apply your positioning consistently.

Internal authority gaps are both a Money Leak and Money Left. They compress pricing power by creating inconsistent client experiences. They create succession risk by making leadership direction unclear to the next generation. And they make international capital invisible to the business by preventing the coherent organizational presentation that sophisticated investors require.

Audit 6 Context
Why This Audit Matters

Internal authority gaps are the IMPACT Pillar's most dangerous incoherence — because they are invisible to the business owner and fully visible to the client.

The business owner experiences the brand they intend. The client experiences the brand they receive — which includes every interaction with every person who represents the business. When those interactions are inconsistent, the client's experience is inconsistent. And an inconsistent experience cannot command consistent premium pricing.

In family businesses, internal authority gaps are also succession signals. When family members operate with different assumptions about what the business stands for and where it is going, those gaps compound at the moment of generational transition into the boardroom conflicts that destroy value.

Audit 6 Diagnosis
Internal Authority

Eight dimensions of internal authority — direction clarity among staff, brand standard application consistency, family member alignment on business identity, client interaction quality equivalence across staff, internal conflict frequency, priority interpretation consistency, leadership communication clarity, and staff ability to articulate business positioning.

If you scored Losing  ·  8–18

Your internal authority is fragmented. Different people in your business operate with different assumptions about what the business is, what it stands for, and how it should present itself. This fragmentation produces an inconsistent client experience — one that cannot support premium pricing because it cannot guarantee a premium standard consistently.

In a family business context, this fragmentation is also a generational risk signal. The authority gaps that exist between founder and team today become the authority conflicts between family members at succession. They do not resolve themselves. They compound.

What to do this week

Ask three people who represent your business — a staff member, a family member involved in the business, and a key partner — to describe what your business stands for and what makes it distinctive. Compare their answers. The variation between them is your internal authority gap. That gap is producing variation in every client interaction.

Within 14 days

Conduct a brand briefing. Bring together everyone who represents the business and share — for the first time explicitly — your positioning, your differentiation, and the standard of impression you expect every interaction to create. A briefing does not produce perfect alignment. But it establishes a shared reference point that did not previously exist.

Within 30 days

Create a simple brand communication guide. One page. What the business stands for. Who it is for. What makes it different. What impression every interaction should create. Give it to everyone who represents the business. Review it quarterly. This is the minimum internal authority architecture.

If you scored Leaking  ·  19–29

Some internal consistency exists. Your core team generally represents the business coherently. The gap is in the edges — the informal interactions, the secondary touchpoints, and the situations where individual interpretation fills the absence of explicit direction.

What to do this week

Identify the most common context in which internal authority breaks down. Is it informal client conversations? Social media representation by staff? Family member communications with suppliers? The specific context determines the specific correction.

Within 14 days

Establish explicit standards for the highest-frequency informal touchpoints. Not a comprehensive brand manual — specific guidance for the specific contexts where variation most often occurs. Targeted guidance produces more immediate improvement than comprehensive guidelines that are never consulted.

Within 30 days

Create a monthly internal brand check-in. One question per month: how are we presenting ourselves in [specific context]? This regular reflection maintains alignment without requiring a significant time investment and surfaces gaps before they become embedded patterns.

If you scored Leveraging  ·  30–40

Your internal authority is strong. The people who represent your business do so consistently and coherently. The internal experience of your brand matches the external positioning you intend. The opportunity at this level is in systematizing what works — converting the alignment that exists because of strong leadership into alignment that exists because of a documented, maintainable system.

What to do now

Document your internal brand standards formally. Alignment that depends on the founder's personal presence to maintain is alignment that does not survive the founder's departure. A documented standard that can be referenced, enforced, and updated is alignment that scales.
